A patch antenna is a small flat metal rectangle printed on a circuit board — the
kind of antenna tucked inside Wi-Fi and Bluetooth devices. It radiates most
strongly at one particular frequency, its resonance. Here we find that resonance
and compare it to the quick textbook estimate.

## The structure

![The patch antenna structure](/rfx/gallery/assets/patch_antenna/geometry.png)

A rectangular metal patch sits on a thin circuit-board substrate above a metal
ground plane, fed from below. The patch size sets the frequency it likes best —
here, near the 2.4 GHz band used by Wi-Fi.

## The result

![Antenna response vs frequency](/rfx/gallery/assets/patch_antenna/sparams.png)

The dip in the curve marks the resonance. The dip sits at 2.66 GHz versus a
2.42 GHz textbook estimate — within about 10%. Read the **location** of the dip,
not how deep it is: the depth depends on fine feed details, but the location is
the meaningful number here.
