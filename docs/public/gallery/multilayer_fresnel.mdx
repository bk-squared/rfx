When a radio wave meets a flat sheet of plastic-like material, part of it bounces
back and part continues through. This is the same effect that makes a window
both reflect and let light pass. Here we measure that split and compare it to the
exact textbook answer.

## The structure

![Cross-section of the dielectric slab](/rfx/gallery/assets/multilayer_fresnel/geometry.png)

A single slab of dielectric material (about four times denser to a radio wave
than air) sits in open space, 10 mm thick. The wave arrives from the left. The
bright band is the slab; everything around it is empty space.

## The result

![Reflection and transmission vs frequency](/rfx/gallery/assets/multilayer_fresnel/sparams.png)

The curves show how much of the wave reflects and how much transmits across the
frequency band. The simulation matches the exact formula to within about 1% —
and the reflected plus transmitted energy adds back up to 100%, as it must for a
lossless material.
