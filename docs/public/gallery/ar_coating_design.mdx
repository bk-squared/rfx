Some materials reflect a lot of the radio waves that hit them, which wastes
energy. A classic fix is to add thin coating layers in front that cancel the
reflection — the same idea as the anti-glare coating on eyeglasses. Here the
computer designs that coating by itself and we check it against exact theory.

## The structure

![The coating stack in front of the substrate](/rfx/gallery/assets/ar_coating_design/geometry.png)

Three thin coating layers sit in front of a strongly reflective surface (shown
bright on the right). Each layer is given a different density, stepping up
gradually so the wave is eased into the surface instead of bouncing off it.

## The physics

<img src="/rfx/gallery/assets/ar_coating_design/fields.gif" alt="Field animation — wave absorbed into the coated surface" width="100%" />

A broadband wave travels toward the coated surface. With the layers in place,
very little reflects back — the wave is absorbed into the surface instead of
echoing toward the source.

## How it was optimized

This is the part that makes rfx unusual: the coating was **designed
automatically**. Starting from a rough guess, the simulator nudges the three
layer densities a little at a time, each step measuring how much the reflection
improves and adjusting accordingly. It is gradient-based design — the simulator
differentiates through its own physics to know which way to push.

![How the design converges](/rfx/gallery/assets/ar_coating_design/convergence.png)

The reflection drops steadily as the design improves, settling near the best
value exact theory says is possible (the dashed line).

![How each layer changes during design](/rfx/gallery/assets/ar_coating_design/design_evolution.png)

Each layer's density shifts during the search and settles close to the
exact-theory target (dashed lines).

## The result

![Reflection before vs after the design](/rfx/gallery/assets/ar_coating_design/result_spectrum.png)

Before the coating, the surface reflects a lot across the whole band. After the
automatic design, the reflection drops dramatically — the computer's design
comes within 1.2× of the exact theoretical optimum.
